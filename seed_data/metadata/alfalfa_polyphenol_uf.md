**DATA-QUALITY CORRECTION (2026-07-05):** This file previously stated "diafiltration removed more than 86% of orthodihydroxy phenolic compounds, yet the dried protein product remained tan-colored rather than white" and framed this as an important NEGATIVE result. Full-text reading of SS-100102349.pdf found NO instance of an "86%" figure anywhere in the paper, and found the paper's own stated conclusion to be the OPPOSITE of "remained tan, not white": the paper explicitly claims to have achieved "a reproducible and characteristic peptidic decolorized permeate" through the combined ultrafiltration + packed-bed column-resin process. This is now the 6th documented data-quality correction in this review's tracking log (after martin2014_spinach's yield [#4] and martin2019_sugarbeet's yield [#5] found earlier in this same session - see literature/needed_pdfs.md for the full running list).

**What the paper actually reports, stage by stage:**
- UF alone (ZrO2 10kDa membrane, pH 9.5, 40C): 51% phenolic reduction, 96% peptide transmission through the membrane - a good peptide-recovery/moderate-decolorization step on its own.
- Batch adsorption screening (10 resins tested): best single-pass compromise between protein loss and decolorization = Amberlite IRA900Cl (an anion exchanger), giving 82.8% protein recovered / 65.9% polyphenols removed (5% resin w/v, pH 5.0, 2h contact, 25C).
- Batch adsorption with resin renewal: substituting fresh resin 3 times raises polyphenol removal to 90%, but protein recovery correspondingly drops to 77% (from 82.8%) - a direct, quantified decolorization-vs-protein-yield trade-off within this single paper.
- Column (packed-bed, continuous) process: outperforms the batch approach - 96% peptide recovery / 92% polyphenol removal simultaneously (i.e., the column geometry avoids the batch trade-off seen above).
- Full pilot-scale process (Table 4, UF followed by column-resin decolorization): starting from 1224 g protein (100%) in the hydrolysate -> permeate 816 g (67% yield after UF) -> discolored permeate 750 g (92% phenolic-EXTRACTION yield relative to the permeate, i.e. 92% of the permeate's phenolics were captured by the resin step) -> **combined overall process: 61% total protein yield, 94% total phenolic extraction yield** (this compounds the two stages' individual efficiencies).

**Why this matters for the review's synthesis (correcting the earlier framing in synthesis_report.md's "Contested" section):** the earlier synthesis used this paper as supporting evidence that "no single mechanism...has been shown to produce a truly colorless leaf protein concentrate," treating alfalfa_polyphenol_uf as a negative/cautionary result alongside duckweed_acs2021's "off-white to light green" (not fully white) outcome. That framing needs to be walked back for THIS paper specifically: its authors' own conclusion is that they DID achieve a "decolorized" product via the combined UF+resin process, at the cost of yield (61% overall, with the deepest decolorization sub-step - 3x resin renewal - costing yield down to 77% recovery at that stage alone). The correct reading is that this paper is a POSITIVE existence proof that polyphenol removal via UF+ion-exchange-resin CAN produce a claimed-decolorized product, at a yield cost, on a substrate that is already a fairly pure protein concentrate (AWPC, ~70% RuBisCO) rather than raw leaf juice - it is not directly comparable to whole-leaf extraction papers in this review, and does not itself measure chlorophyll (its whole framing is about polyphenols, a related but mechanistically distinct color/off-flavor contributor to chlorophyll).

**Practical implication for carrot process design:** this paper is best read as a candidate POLISHING step (applied after a primary protein concentrate has already been produced) rather than a primary extraction method - i.e. a two-stage strategy of (1) get RuBisCO out of the leaf matrix by whatever primary method works on carrot, THEN (2) apply a UF+ion-exchange-resin polishing step modeled on this paper specifically to address residual polyphenol-driven off-color/off-flavor, separately from whatever addresses chlorophyll. This is consistent with cloverbiorefinery2025's two-stage architecture (though that paper targets chlorophyll-binding proteins specifically, not free phenolics) and reinforces gaps_and_opportunities.md's observation that color/off-flavor removal may require multiple, mechanistically distinct steps rather than one single intervention.
