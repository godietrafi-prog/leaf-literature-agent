Open access (PMC), full text obtained. This is the most carrot-directly-relevant and highest-priority finding of the entire scoping pass. Root cause identified precisely: for carrot and cabbage, "less than 30% of the protein content in green juice was found in the particle-free supernatant after centrifugation," versus >50% (up to 80%+ for spinach/lucerne) for the 7 successful crops - i.e. carrot/cabbage protein does not separate from particulate matter in the green juice the way other leaf proteins do, so the downstream thermal/acid precipitation steps have very little free protein left to work with by the time they're applied. This is a disruption/solubilization-stage failure, not a precipitation-stage failure - meaning the fix likely needs to happen BEFORE precipitation (e.g. enzymatic cell-wall/particle breakdown per radish_fh2025, or a fundamentally different disruption method), not by tuning pH or temperature at the precipitation step, which is what most of the rest of the literature in this pass optimizes. The paper's own conclusion explicitly recommends "a general fractionation procedure where parameters are easily adjusted based on biomass type should instead be developed" rather than applying one fixed protocol everywhere - directly supporting this project's premise that a genuinely new, carrot-adapted (not just borrowed) protocol is needed. Also notable: Beta vulgaris varieties (sugarbeet, beetroot, mangold) showed low thermal stability with a sharp protein drop at 55C, while Brassica-family species (kale, broccoli, cabbage) were more heat-stable - carrot (Apiaceae family, not tested for thermal stability specifically since it failed at the juice-separation stage before thermal treatment even mattered) may behave differently again; this is unknown and worth testing directly.
