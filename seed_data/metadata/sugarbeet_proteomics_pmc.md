Open access (PMC), full text obtained. 817 total proteins identified across all 5 methods combined (81% found in common - 661 proteins - meaning the methods largely agree on presence/absence, but disagree on relative abundance of the ones that matter). The key actionable number: ammonium sulfate precipitation (AS) gives the HIGHEST RuBisCO relative abundance (35.81%) of the 5 methods tested, while heat coagulation (HC) gives the LOWEST (16.65%) - more than double the difference - despite total protein content being "comparable" across methods per the abstract. High-pressure-assisted methods (ISOHP, HCHP) show elevated recovery of chloroplast-resident, chlorophyll-binding proteins specifically (via homogenizer-driven cell wall disruption at 1400 bar), which is a double-edged finding: it improves access to the RuBisCO-containing chloroplast fraction, but chlorophyll-binding proteins are exactly what needs to be separated OUT for the color target, not just recovered. Directly actionable for this project: (1) ammonium sulfate precipitation is worth testing on carrot leaf specifically as a RuBisCO-enrichment lever that doesn't require heat or acid; (2) any process design should include a proteomic or at least electrophoretic (native/SDS-PAGE) readout, not just a gravimetric protein assay, since "% protein" and "% RuBisCO of that protein" are shown here to vary independently by an order more than expected.
