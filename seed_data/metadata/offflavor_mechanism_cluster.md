**UPDATE (2026-07-05):** Vatansever, Chen & Hall (2024) full text now obtained and read in full (25 pages, "Sustainable Food Proteins - 2024 - Vatansever..." PDF, correct filename located after two initial filename-guess failures). This significantly expands what was previously only a citation-level entry.

**Scope caveat, important for this review:** this paper is explicitly scoped to pulses (pea, lentil, chickpea), soybean, and oilseeds (flaxseed, hempseed) - the authors state directly "other plant proteins exist, they will not be covered extensively in this limited review." Leaf proteins (RuBisCO-based) are NOT discussed anywhere in this paper. It should therefore be used in this review ONLY as a source of general flavor-chemistry MECHANISM and mitigation-METHOD evidence, not as a source of any leaf/RuBisCO-specific number - no purity/yield/chlorophyll data exists in this paper to extract, and none should be inferred or carried over from its pea/soy numbers into a leaf-protein context.

**VOCs (volatile organic compounds) - the "aroma" half of off-flavor:** major classes are aldehydes, ketones, furans, and alkyl methoxypyrazines. Two large descriptor tables (Table 1: ~80 individual volatiles with sensory descriptors; Table 2: same volatiles cross-referenced by source species) catalog compounds like hexanal (grass/floral/pea/vegetable), 1-octen-3-ol (mushroom/vegetable/earthy/green), and alkyl methoxypyrazines (intense "green pea"/"bell pepper" aroma at very low odor threshold - among the most potent single off-flavor contributors identified). Lipoxygenase (LOX)-promoted oxidation of polyunsaturated fatty acids is identified as the dominant VOC-generating mechanism, producing conjugated fatty acid hydroperoxides that decompose to hexanal, 1-hexanol, and 3-hexenal among others - this is the same LOX pathway already anchoring this review's off-flavor mechanism note, now with much more specific compound-level detail.

**Non-VOCs - the "taste" half (bitterness, astringency):** saponins (glycosides, e.g. soyasaponins), phenolic acids and flavonoids (chlorogenic acid, quercetin, rutin, kaempferol derivatives), and bitter peptides/cyclolinopeptides. Human bitter taste receptors (hTAS2R14, hTAS2R39, and 23 other subtypes) have been used analytically to assess bitterness activation by specific compounds - e.g. genistein activates hTAS2R14 at just 4-8 uM. Polyphenol-protein binding occurs via hydrogen bonding, ionic interactions, covalent bonding (Schiff base/Michael's-type adducts via oxidized quercetin/rutin), and hydrophobic interactions - mechanistically the same binding chemistry already invoked in this review's carrot_composition_baseline/alfalfa_polyphenol_uf discussion of polyphenol-protein co-extraction, now with explicit reaction-mechanism diagrams (Figure 3 of the paper).

**Binding reversibility - the key practical distinction:** interactions via carboxyl (-COOH), sulfhydryl (-SH), amine (-NH2), and hydroxyl (-OH) groups are REVERSIBLE; disulfide (-S-S-) bond formation is IRREVERSIBLE. Practical implication: "any covalently bound flavor to protein will not be removed by current technologies without substantial changes to the protein structure" - i.e., prevention (targeting the disruption/grinding step, before oxidation locks flavors in via covalent bonds) is more tractable than post-hoc removal once irreversible binding has occurred. This directly reinforces gaps_and_opportunities.md's item #7 (test a brief pre-emptive blanching/heat-inactivation step before disruption).

**Mitigation methods ranked by the review's own practicality assessment:**
1. **Solvent extraction (the review's top recommendation)**: aqueous ethanol at 35-75% vol/vol is repeatedly shown effective (e.g., 47.5% ethanol/63min extraction improved cookie sensory scores made with treated pea flour; SC-CO2+22% ethanol at 86C/42.71 MPa reduced pea flour bitterness). The review explicitly justifies this choice on PRACTICAL grounds, not just efficacy: ethanol is cheap, needs no specialized equipment beyond what a protein-isolation facility already has (mixing tanks, centrifuges/decanters), and has minimal regulatory residue limits (21 CFR 173.210 covers ethanol with few restrictions, vs. tighter ppm limits for acetone/methanol/isopropanol).
2. **Adsorbent/ion-exchange resins**: beta-cyclodextrin removed 94% of bound 2-nonanone from soy protein; Sepabeads SP207 removed 30-41% of hexanal from soy protein and 32-41% of aldehydes from lentil protein isolate; combined adsorbent+anion-exchange resin reduced sunflower protein phenolics by 99% in a pilot-scale prep - directly comparable in mechanism (though different resin/target) to alfalfa_polyphenol_uf's Amberlite IRA900Cl approach already in this review.
3. **Protein modification**: succinylation and deamidation reduce flavor-binding affinity in some cases but can also increase it for other flavor compounds (mechanism-specific, not uniformly beneficial) - a caution against assuming any single modification is a universal fix.
4. **Germination/fermentation**: inconsistent and crop-specific - can reduce SOME off-flavors (chickpea) while INCREASING others via new volatile formation (lentil, lupin) - confirms this review's existing framing that fermentation/biopurification is a real but imperfect, crop-specific lever, not a universal solution.
5. **Thermal/hydrothermal (blanching, heat-steam)**: effective at reducing beany/off aromas in precooked flours and improves sensory scores, but "not suitable for deflavoring protein ingredients due to the impact on functionality" for isolated protein specifically (as opposed to whole flour) - an important nuance: heat treatment may work at the whole-leaf/juice stage (consistent with furia2025's carrot-specific "less green but more cooked" finding) but is explicitly cautioned against for a purified isolate.
6. **Filtration/membrane methods**: the review notes this is the area with the LEAST published flavor-characterization data ("this area of research should be expanded") despite membrane methods (UF/DF) being widely used for protein isolation generally - i.e. this review found essentially no literature connecting membrane-based purification (the same method family as cloverbiorefinery2025 and duckweed_acs2021, both central to this project) to flavor outcomes specifically, aside from a couple of pea-protein studies (Benavides-Paz et al. 2022) showing modest volatile reduction after UF/DF. This is a genuine, named gap in the field, not just an artifact of this review's own search - worth flagging explicitly in gaps_and_opportunities.md as validated by an independent, comprehensive flavor-chemistry review.

**Overall conclusion of the Vatansever review, directly relevant to this project's framing:** off-flavor mitigation strategy must be chosen based on WHETHER the binding is reversible or irreversible, and the review's own top pick (aqueous ethanol solvent extraction) is recommended primarily for cost/practicality reasons over efficacy alone - a useful benchmark against which any future carrot-specific off-flavor mitigation step should be compared, since ethanol washing could plausibly be tested on carrot leaf juice/protein at minimal additional process complexity.
