Open access (PMC), full text obtained. Establishes ~29 tons/hectare of unused sugar beet leaf biomass annually (22.8% crude protein by dry matter) as the scale of the valorization opportunity this type of research addresses generally. Key findings: leaf position matters (inner leaves have highest total protein at 31.6 g/kg but lowest extractability ~40%; outer leaves lowest protein at 21.8 g/kg but highest extractability ~55%; middle leaves are the practical compromise, most stable for sampling) - this is a genetics/physiology-level variable that could apply to carrot tops too (top leaves vs. inner leaves of the same plant may extract differently) and is worth checking before assuming "carrot leaf" is a uniform input. NIR spectroscopy predicts total protein content excellently (R²=0.97) but predicts EXTRACTABILITY poorly (R²=0.00-0.24) - meaning total protein content and how much of it is actually extractable are genuinely different, not-well-correlated properties, reinforcing that "measure total protein" is not a substitute for "measure what you can actually get out." Sample-size guidance given (27-36 plants needed for reliable yield/extractability measurement) is directly reusable if this lab wants to screen multiple carrot cultivars/lines. Most directly useful sentence in the paper for this project: the authors themselves state their methods "can aid measurement of leaf protein extraction in sugar beet, but potentially also other crops where leaves are a byproduct, such as potato and carrot" - i.e. this is a ready-made, published, low-cost screening method this lab could adopt directly for carrot cultivar screening (see gaps_and_opportunities.md item on cultivar selection).
