**What the paper actually does (verified):** Kannapinn, Dorer, Schäfer & Weeger, *Journal of Food Engineering* 410 (2026) 112918, DOI 10.1016/j.jfoodeng.2025.112918, open access (CC BY), TU Darmstadt. It builds a digital-twin-based MPC framework for autonomous thermal food processing (cooking chicken fillet in a convection oven to target core temperature / moisture / texture). The digital twin is a physics-based, data-driven reduced-order model of **augmented neural ODE (ANODE)** type, trained on trajectories from a high-fidelity multiphysics finite-element model. The ANODE-ROM achieves 0.18–0.49% relative time-series error and runs ~36,000× faster than real time; the full control algorithm evaluates 501 trajectories of 1800 s process time in 46.6 s on a single CPU core — i.e. on-device, no cloud. The central novelty is a sub-optimization step that re-synchronizes the ANODE surrogate to the *measured* state at each control step, fixing ANODEs' fixed-initial-condition limitation for MPC.

**Two honest nuances that matter for how we USE this precedent (and that actually strengthen our own novelty):**
1. **It is a VIRTUAL / in-silico study.** No physical oven was available; the "physical" process was itself mocked by a validated 3D multiphysics simulation. So the paper proves the neural-ODE-digital-twin-MPC *method* works in simulation — it does NOT yet demonstrate it on real, noisy sensor data from a real process. **Our project would apply the neural-ODE idea to real DGMA sensor data — a step beyond this paper's regime.**
2. **Their ANODE is trained on high-fidelity physics-simulation data, not on sparse experimental data.** They have a first-principles finite-element model to generate as much clean training data as they want; the ANODE just learns a fast surrogate of that known physics. **We have no such first-principles simulator of LOX-kinetics + downstream processing — we would fit the neural ODE directly to limited, noisy experimental DGMA+GC-MS pairs.** That is a harder, more data-constrained regime, which means our modeling contribution is not merely "applying an off-the-shelf method" — it extends the neural-ODE-for-food-process idea into the real-experimental-sparse-data setting the precedent did not attempt.
