Full text obtained - this is a genuine confirmation case (not a correction): the previously recorded 57.6% purity / 60.0% yield / ~85% RuBisCO figures all check out against the actual paper. This raises modest confidence in the earlier (pre-full-text) WebFetch extractions generally, though the pattern from other papers this session (6 corrections found) still argues for verifying before relying on any single-source number.

**Major new finding - the byproduct is purer than the product:** proteomics (Table 8) shows the purification supernatant (PS, i.e. what stays dissolved and is normally discarded as "brown juice") is 89.6-92.1% RuBisCO by weight, HIGHER than the purification pellet (PP, the intended product) at 84.5-85.6%. The authors hypothesize this is because RuBisCO's small (S) subunits stay soluble near pH4 while large (L) subunits and non-RuBisCO membrane proteins precipitate preferentially - meaning the "waste" stream from a pH4 precipitation process may be a purer RuBisCO source than the main pellet, just at lower concentration. **This deserves a direct check on any carrot-leaf pH-shift experiment** - measure both fractions, not just the pellet, before discarding the supernatant.

**Explicit reliability caveat (from the authors themselves, not just this reviewer's caution):** digestion yields for the proteomics quantification were high for the initial powder (50.5%) but LOW for both PP (9.6-10.7%) and PS (1.5-1.7%) samples - the authors explicitly write "cautious conclusions should be drawn from the results" for the %RuBisCO figures on the purified fractions. Treat the 84.5-92.1% figures as directionally right but not to be over-interpreted at the decimal level.

**Contested finding vs. duckweed_acs2021 (Nieuwland et al. 2021) - add to synthesis "Contested" section:** this paper (Muller et al. 2023) uses isoelectric precipitation as ITS chosen, successful method (57.6%/60%, described as producing good foaming properties - "superior foaming capacity... 194% vs. egg white's 122%"). But Nieuwland et al. 2021 (duckweed_acs2021) explicitly REJECTED isoelectric precipitation on the same general system (also duckweed) because it produced an insoluble, functionally dead product. Both papers are from closely related Northern-European duckweed-protein research lineages (both cite Tenorio, Martin, and each other's antecedents) and reach opposite conclusions about whether pH-4 precipitation preserves duckweed protein functionality. Possible reconciling factors not yet checked: different duckweed species/source (Lemna gibba vs. this paper's supplier powder), different exact pH-lowering rate/method, or different post-precipitation handling. This is a genuine, citable contradiction - worth flagging to the user rather than silently picking one paper's conclusion.

Includes a carrot data point: **carrot IEP at pH4.5 = 0.2% total protein yield / 1.1% protein purity.** CORRECTION (2026-07-05, #7): this is NOT an independent confirmation of carrot_greenleaves_pmc - reference [21] in this paper's own bibliography is "Nynäs, A.-L.; Newson, W.R.; Johansson, E. Foods 2021, 10, 2533" - i.e. the EXACT SAME paper as carrot_greenleaves_pmc (PMC8622718), just quoted in a comparison table rather than independently re-tested. The carrot fractionation-failure finding in this review rests on ONE study (one lab, one paper, 9 crops tested together), not two. Treat as a strong, specific, quantified hypothesis - not as independently replicated science. Cabbage similarly poor (0.2%/0.7%). For contrast, in the same table: cassava 48.7%/50.0% (surprisingly good - contradicts the mixed cassava results found elsewhere in this review, worth reconciling), Moringa 7.3%/26.9%, sugar beet up to 32.7%/~59%, alfalfa up to 36%/60%. This table should be treated as a secondary/tertiary source (citing others' numbers, not independently re-verified here) but is a useful single-place cross-species purity/yield-via-IEP comparison - added to protocol_matrix.md.
