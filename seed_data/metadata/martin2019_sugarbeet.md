**DATA-QUALITY CORRECTION (2026-07-05):** This file previously recorded `yield_pct: 11` (citation-level guess, never independently verified). Full-text reading of the paper found the explicit statement: "The extraction yield of isolated RuBisCO starting from 15.2 kg of leaves (without stem) was 50 g (3 g kg-1) based on the wet mass of the leaves" - i.e. 50 g final RuBisCO protein isolate (RPI) from 15.2 kg fresh leaf = **3 g/kg fresh weight = 0.33% yield**, not 11%. The paper itself notes this is "close to that of 4 g/kg of water-soluble leaf protein concentrate using grasslands reported by Ostrowski-Meissner" - i.e. the authors themselves frame 0.3-0.4% as a normal/expected order of magnitude for this kind of high-purity isolate, not an anomaly. This is now the 5th documented data-quality correction in this review's tracking log (4th being martin2014_spinach's yield in this same session - see literature/needed_pdfs.md correction log for the full list).

Purity note: the paper's own abstract states "930 g/kg crude protein" but Table 1 (proximate analysis of the RPI) reports "Protein (N x 5.8): 864.0 g/kg" as the directly measured figure, alongside non-protein N <17.0 g/kg, moisture 58.0 g/kg, ash <10.0 g/kg, lipid 1.6 g/kg, total fiber 57.0 g/kg. The 86.4% figure (864.0 g/kg) already recorded in protocol_matrix.md and this file is the correct, Table-1-sourced number and did NOT need correction - only the yield figure was wrong.

Process clarification (important methodological finding): unlike martin2014_spinach's chromatography-based (Q-Sepharose + Sephadex G75) purification route, this sugar-beet RuBisCO isolation uses NO chromatography step at all in production - only heat coagulation (50C/15min, to precipitate/remove chlorophyll-bound material via subsequent centrifugation), centrifugation, ultrafiltration (100 kDa cutoff), and two-stage diafiltration. Chromatography (SEC on Superdex 200, SDS-PAGE) is used ONLY for analytical characterization of the finished product, not as a purification step. This is the more industrially "scalable" route the abstract refers to, and achieves nearly the same purity (86.4% vs 95%) as the chromatography-heavy spinach method at presumably much lower cost/complexity - though at an even lower yield (0.33% vs 1.0%), reinforcing that purity and yield trade off sharply even within the same research group's own two best papers.

Functional properties (benchmarked directly against commercial WPI and SPI): RPI solubility >80% below pH 4.0 or above pH 5.5 (pI 4.6-5.5); RPI forms self-supporting gels at much lower protein concentration (critical gelation concentration <2.5%) than WPI or SPI need; at pH 7.0, 50 g/kg RPI solution formed a self-supporting gel while WPI needed 130 g/kg and SPI needed 100 g/kg to do the same - i.e. RuBisCO from sugar beet is a substantially more efficient gelling agent by weight than either commercial benchmark. Foaming: RPI overrun >85% at 10 g/kg concentration at pH 4.0, better than WPI or SPI at the same concentration; foam stability also higher than WPI/SPI at pH 4.0. Emulsions: RPI produced small, stable oil droplets (d32 <2 um), comparable to WPI, and visually white (not off-color) unlike SPI's yellow-tinted emulsions. Overall conclusion: RuBisCO from sugar beet leaves has functional properties "comparable or superior" to commercial whey and soy isolates, supporting its potential as a food ingredient despite the very low yield.

Companion/benchmark paper: martin2014_spinach (same TNO/Nestle research group, same general functional-characterization approach, chromatography-based on spinach instead of heat+UF-based on sugar beet).
