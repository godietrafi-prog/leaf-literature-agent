**Why this is the standout paper of the whole review:** the full 3x3 combination table (Table 2) shows THREE separate conditions that independently clear both this project's purity band and yield/recovery target at once:
- **5S-NF (5 min sonication, no filtration): 50.76% purity / 47.52% recovery** - inside the purity band, well above the yield target.
- **NS-F124 (no sonication, 124um filter): 61.89% purity / 37.81% recovery** - above the purity band, above the yield target.
- **NS-NF (no sonication, no filter, i.e. simplest possible condition): 57.16% purity / 39.19% recovery** - inside the purity band, above the yield target.

No other paper in this entire review (including cloverbiorefinery2025, duckweed_pmc2023, moringa_gels2025, or any of the high-purity chromatography/patent routes) has THREE independently verified conditions clearing both numeric targets at once - most others hit one target at the severe expense of the other (see the purity/yield frontier established across this review: 95% purity spinach at 1.0% yield; 86.4% at 0.33%; 57.7% clover-grass at 7%). This makes cauliflower's simple sonication+IEP protocol the single strongest existing template to adapt directly to carrot leaves.

**Important nuances:**
- The relationship is NOT simply "more sonication = better." Longer sonication (10min) INCREASES dry-matter yield (up to 20.45%) but DECREASES protein purity (down to 44.53-48.39%) - a classic trade-off. The best purity/recovery combinations found (5S-NF, NS-F124, NS-NF) all use LESS or NO sonication, contradicting the common assumption that more aggressive disruption is always better.
- Finer sieve filtration (F48) generally reduces both yield and recovery slightly (removes more material) while sometimes increasing purity modestly - a secondary, weaker lever than sonication time.
- Color: darkest product (lowest L*) was 10S-F48 (most sonication + finest filter) - i.e. the condition that gives the WORST purity/yield also gives the WORST color. The BEST purity/yield conditions (NS/5S with no or coarse filtration) were NOT explicitly reported as the lightest either - color and purity/yield don't obviously co-optimize in this paper's data, needs the full color table cross-check before assuming the best-performing condition is also acceptably light-colored.
- No chlorophyll-specific or off-flavor measurement at all - this paper's excellent purity/yield numbers come with a gap on exactly the two other target axes (color, flavor) this project cares about.
- Uses a nitrogen-to-protein factor of 5.4 (not the generic 6.25) "as commonly utilised for biomass protein analysis" - citing Ducrocq et al. 2022 and Nissen et al. 2022 - worth using the same factor if directly comparing to carrot-leaf work, since a lower factor slightly deflates purity% relative to 6.25-based papers.
- FTIR/DSC/SEM structural characterization included: denaturation peaks at 61.50-63°C (in line with duckweed's ~61°C and broccoli/cabbage LPC's 60.30-64.66°C - i.e. leaf RuBisCO-family denaturation temperature is fairly consistent ~60-65°C across species). Protein solubility lowest at pH4 (8-16%), highest at pH9 (17-32%) - consistent isoelectric-point behavior seen elsewhere in this review.
- Companion paper by the same group (Furia et al. 2025b, cited as ref, "Leaf protein concentrate from cauliflower leaves: Effect of filtration and stabilizing solution on extraction yield and physio-chemical properties", Innovative Food Sci Emerging Technol 103:104037) appears to be a direct precursor study - NOT yet obtained, worth adding to a future needed-papers pass if deeper cauliflower detail is wanted.
