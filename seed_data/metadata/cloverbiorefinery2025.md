**VERIFICATION NOTE (2026-07-05):** This is the highest-priority paper in the entire review per task instructions. Full text of the RSC-published version (d5fb00771b.pdf) was read directly; the preprint was not needed as a gap-filler since the RSC version was complete and detailed. The earlier (pre-full-text) metadata draft had `protein_purity_pct: null` with a note "not simple purity - see note" and `chlorophyll_removal_pct: >99.9%` (which held up exactly) but no yield number at all. Both numbers are now filled in directly from Table 1 of the paper: **DF C (final diafiltered concentrate = the food product stream) had crude protein 57.70 +/- 0.86% (DM basis, N-to-protein factor 6.25)**, confirming the 57.70% figure that had already appeared (apparently correctly guessed or partially sourced) in protocol_matrix.md's frontier table before this session.

**Yield is genuinely modest, not the implied "near-total retention" the abstract's qualitative language might suggest.** The paper explicitly states: "Ultimately, around 7% of initial protein (by CP) ended up in the final product stream. Ultimately, this yield was in fact the lowest of the production season, where the CP yields in DF C range from approximately 7% to 15%." The authors chose this specific (lowest-yield) batch for full characterization purely because it had the most complete set of subsamples available across all process streams, not because it was representative of the best achievable yield - so 7% should be read as a conservative, verified floor, with the true achievable range being 7-15% (upper bound not independently re-verified in this paper; described as "manuscript in preparation" for the full-season dataset).

**Why yield is capped at 7-15%:** substantial protein losses occur across the whole process, not concentrated at one step. ~60% of protein (by CP) is retained in the fiber/pulp fraction during the initial screw-press wet fractionation (never enters the liquid stream at all) - this alone caps the theoretical ceiling. A further ~25% of nitrogen is lost as small peptides/non-protein-N in the downstream permeate streams (S2 perm + DF perm) during the second filtration/diafiltration stage. So of the protein that DOES enter the green juice, only a fraction survives both the pulp-loss step and the two membrane stages to reach the final concentrate.

**Mechanistic finding (the core contribution of the paper):** using MS-based bottom-up proteomics (MaxLFQ + riBAQ quantification) across every process stream, the paper shows that the >99.9% pigment removal is caused by highly selective retention of specific PIGMENT-BINDING and MEMBRANE-ASSOCIATED proteins in the FIRST membrane stage (60 nm ceramic membrane) alone - not by the second stage or diafiltration. Specifically: chlorophyll a-b binding protein (CBP/LHCB, from T. subterraneum, T. repens, L. perenne) was the single most enriched protein group in the Stage-1 retentate (log2 fold-change 8.9-10.4), while a distinct ~25 kDa SDS-PAGE band (visually tracking the green color) disappears entirely downstream of Stage 1. CBP abundance dropped from 5.9% of S1-feed protein to <0.03% in S1 permeate (>99% removal already at Stage 1), then further to <0.002% in the final product (DF C) after Stage 2 + diafiltration (>99.9% total). Photosystem I/II proteins (PI/PII, also pigment-associated) were similarly depleted from 7.6% (S1 feed) to 0.06% (DF C), a >99% removal. In contrast, RuBisCO (rbc) showed NO depletion at Stage 1 (~20% of protein in all samples upstream of DF) and was, if anything, slightly enriched downstream - the process is selective FOR RuBisCO retention and AGAINST chlorophyll-binding/membrane protein retention, at the same time, via simple size/membrane-association-based separation rather than any protein-specific chemistry.

**Allergen-safety finding:** non-specific lipid transfer proteins (nsLTPs), a well-known class of plant food allergens, were depleted >90% by the whole process (2.15% in S1 perm down to 0.21% in the final product), and reticuline oxidase-like protein (also linked to pollen-allergy cross-reactivity in some contexts) dropped to <0.004% in the final product, "not considered of particular relevance at the bulk level" per the authors.

**Antioxidant-enrichment finding:** proteins with known antioxidant function - thioredoxins (TRX-M, TRX-H), superoxide dismutase (SOD), and glutathione S-transferase (GST) - were found enriched in the final product stream relative to upstream, a potential added-value/health-positioning angle beyond pure purity/yield numbers.

**RuBisCO content caveat directly relevant to carrot:** the paper explicitly notes rbc constituted only ~20% of biomass protein and ~26% of raw-juice protein in THIS specific batch, "somewhat lower than expected" since RuBisCO is reported to constitute 30-50% of protein in fresh green juice from L. perenne in a previous study by the same group, and 50%+ of soluble leaf protein in general clover/grass literature. The authors attribute the shortfall to either (a) natural batch-to-batch variation, or (b) an unusually long (up to double the normal) storage time between harvest and processing for this batch, which may have allowed partial proteolytic degradation of rbc in the green juice before processing began - i.e. even within this single, best-in-class paper, RuBisCO content is not fixed and is sensitive to post-harvest handling time, a directly transferable caution for any carrot-leaf protocol design (minimize time between harvest and processing).

**No heat, no acid, no chromatography used anywhere in this process** - this is the key structural difference from both martin2014_spinach (chromatography) and martin2019_sugarbeet (heat coagulation step) and is the paper's central claim to being "non-destructive": pH stays near-native throughout, temperature never exceeds 35C, and separation is achieved purely by cross-flow membrane filtration at two different pore-size/MW cutoffs (60 nm ceramic first, then 10 kDa polymeric) plus diafiltration - i.e. a physical size/association-based separation, not a chemical or thermal one. This directly supports gaps_and_opportunities.md's item #2 recommendation to test membrane-based routes on carrot as a default hypothesis instead of heat/acid precipitation.
