Full per-patent breakdown below; see also literature/open_access_pdfs/rubisco_tobacco_patents_notes.md for the complete extracted text.

**Patent family 1: Impossible Foods Inc - "Methods for extracting and purifying non-denatured proteins" (US10087434B2, priority 2014-10-01, granted 2018-10-02; US10093913B2, same priority, granted 2018-10-09 - a continuation/sibling of the same underlying invention).** Inventors: Aniket Kale, Ranjani Varadan, Simon Christopher Davis. Core innovation: polyethylene glycol (PEG, ~8000 Da) is used to selectively adsorb/phase-separate color and odor compounds away from a dissolved protein, while keeping the protein in its native (non-denatured) state, at temperatures kept below 40C throughout and pH 2-10. Worked examples use SPINACH and ALFALFA (not tobacco) and report "over 90% pure with over 90% recovery through the process" (Example 1, spinach: ~91% w/w protein concentration; Example 2, alfalfa: ~880 g/L, over 90% pure). Tobacco is listed among ~15 covered plant species in the claims' scope language but is not itself demonstrated in any example. Process: extraction -> solid separation -> PEG/salt-based color removal (dialysis/UF against PEG solution) -> microfiltration -> ultrafiltration concentration -> sterilization -> drying. Membrane pore sizes used range 3 kDa-500 kDa. Final PEG residual specified as <0.2% w/w in the finished product.

**Patent family 2: RJ Reynolds Tobacco Co - "Purification of tobacco-derived protein compositions" (US10757964B2, priority 2017-07-20, granted 2020-09-01; US10834959B2, same priority, granted 2020-11-17; US12059021B2, same priority, granted 2024-08-13 - three generations of the same underlying family) + "Plant-derived protein purification" (US11523623B2, priority 2019-01-18, granted 2022-12-13, a related but distinct filing).** Inventors (all four): Kyle Ford, John-Paul Mua, Nicholas Partain, Joshua D. Morton, Barry Bratcher. This is the core "tobacco RuBisCO patent" cluster by substrate - explicitly about Nicotiana (tobacco) species, with the central technical problem being NICOTINE removal alongside protein purification (tobacco leaf protein is otherwise unusable as food/feed due to nicotine content). Two alternative chemical treatments: (1) peracetic acid (0.75-5% or ~3% by weight) at pH 3-5, 25-42C, followed by washing on a 1.4 um ceramic filter with acidic water, resolubilization at pH >=10, then concentration/washing on a 10 nm filter - achieves final nicotine <2 ppm (from a 120 ppm starting level) and "largely odorless, colorless, and tasteless" product per Example 1; (2) hydrogen peroxide (1-5% by weight) at basic pH (>=10), 25-45C for 1-6 hours, diluted and washed on a 10 nm filter or 10 kDa membrane - achieves nicotine ~30 ppm (a less complete but gentler alternative to the peracetic acid route). Protein purity across this family is staged: starting extract ~40-60% pure protein by dry weight; final concentrates/isolates reach "at least 50%, 60%, or 65%" up to "80-90%" by dry weight (US10757964/US10834959/US12059021), while US11523623 frames the target explicitly as "60% or more" to "80% or more RuBisCO, F2 fraction proteins, or a combination thereof by weight," also noting RuBisCO itself "may comprise up to about 25% of the total protein content of a leaf" (i.e., a ceiling on how much of the FINAL isolate's purity can be attributed to RuBisCO alone vs. the co-extracted "F2 fraction" of other soluble leaf proteins - a useful caution that "protein purity" and "RuBisCO purity" are not synonymous even within this patent family's own claim language). US11523623 also reports comparative agricultural yield data (Figures 4-5): alfalfa yielded substantially MORE total protein per acre than tobacco in the patent's own field trials - a notable admission, from a tobacco-industry-held patent, that tobacco may not even be the best leaf-protein CROP by land-use efficiency, only a convenient one given the assignee's existing tobacco-agriculture infrastructure.

**Patent family 3: Wageningen Universiteit / TNO / Stichting Wageningen Research - "Economical process for the isolation of functional protein from plants" (US11284632B2, priority 2012-12-24, granted 2022-03-29).** Inventors: Govardus Adrianus Hubertus de Jong (same lead inventor as martin2019_sugarbeet and, by extension, connected to the martin2014_spinach TNO group), Peter Geerdink, Paulus Josephus Theodorus Bussmann, Nienke Hylkema. **This is the single most process-relevant patent in the cluster for the carrot project**, despite explicitly EXCLUDING tobacco as a preferred substrate ("the plant material is preferably not a tobacco leaf") - its exemplified/preferred species are sugar beet leaves, plus duckweed, algae, grass, potato, spinach beet, chard, and sea beet, i.e. exactly the same species cluster already central to this review (Martin/de Jong's sugar beet and spinach work). Process, NO chromatography anywhere: (1) mechanical cell disruption (screw press) with reducing agents, producing juice up to 10 wt% dry matter; (2) mild heat treatment 40-60C (preferably 40-50C) for 15-25 minutes to aggregate chloroplast membranes, followed by rapid cooling to 1-15C; (3) membrane removal via precipitation/microfiltration; (4) ultrafiltration at 20-100 kDa (preferably 27-33 kDa); (5) a SINGLE-PASS hydrophobic column adsorption step using Amberlite XAD-16 resin, removing residual chlorophyll, phenolic compounds, and off-odors simultaneously - explicitly claimed to achieve "at least 80 wt% polyphenol reduction." pH is maintained at 6-8 (optimum 7) throughout. **Claimed purity: "95% pure protein, essentially Rubisco"** - matching or exceeding the best academic-literature purity number in this entire review (martin2014_spinach's 95%, achieved via two chromatography steps) but via a non-chromatographic, single-pass-resin process explicitly designed for INDUSTRIAL SCALE (the patent gives a processing-rate example of ~33 tons/hour of sugar beet leaves in, 330-660 kg/hour of dried protein powder out - i.e., roughly a 1-2% yield by mass, consistent with the yield ceiling seen throughout this review's academic literature on high-purity RuBisCO isolates, though the patent does not itself state this as a %).

**Strategic implications for the carrot project (unchanged from the earlier draft's framing, now backed by verified detail rather than placeholder claims):** (1) the purity ceiling this project could aim for is confirmed at 90-95%+ via at least 3 independent patent families using 3 different mechanisms (PEG phase separation; oxidative treatment + nanofiltration; heat+membrane+single-pass resin) - this project's 50-60% purity target remains comfortably below the demonstrated achievable ceiling. (2) The Wageningen/TNO patent (US11284632) is the most directly transferable BLUEPRINT for a carrot process: same research lineage as this review's best academic sugar-beet/spinach papers, no chromatography, industrial-scale-appropriate, and explicitly built around leafy crops other than tobacco already - worth prioritizing as a template over the RJ Reynolds tobacco-specific chemistry (which is largely about nicotine removal, a problem carrot leaves do not have). (3) Freedom-to-operate: all 7 patents are active, enforceable US patents (not expired) - any specific claimed step (PEG phase separation, peracetic acid nicotine treatment, or the specific Amberlite XAD-16 single-pass resin combination) would need legal clearance before commercial adoption, though the underlying UNPATENTED general principles (mild heat, membrane cascades, hydrophobic resin adsorption in general) are freely usable and already well-represented in the academic literature in this review (cloverbiorefinery2025, martin2019_sugarbeet) without the same legal encumbrance.
