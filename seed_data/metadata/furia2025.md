Full text obtained and read (pages 1-34). This is the master review referenced throughout protocol_matrix.md and gaps_and_opportunities.md; it was previously listed in needed_pdfs.md as paywalled (403 on tandfonline) but was supplied directly by the user as a PDF.

**Structure:** the review is organized by extraction-method family (heat coagulation, pH-shift/isoelectric precipitation, membrane/UF, ultrasound-assisted extraction, enzyme-assisted extraction, microwave-assisted extraction, pulsed electric field), with each section covering mechanism, typical parameter ranges, and reported purity/yield outcomes across many species (alfalfa, spinach, duckweed, sugar beet, moringa, various leafy vegetables) - broadly the same taxonomy already used in this project's own protocol_matrix.md, which is reassuring cross-validation of the synthesis structure chosen for this review rather than a sign that anything in protocol_matrix.md needs restructuring.

**What furia2025 covers that this review's protocol_matrix.md/synthesis_report.md did NOT already have:**
1. **Table 3 (antinutritional compounds cross-reference)**: a species x compound-class matrix covering phenolics, polyphenol oxidase (PPO) activity, cyanogenic compounds, saponins, tannins, lignin, oxalates, and endogenous protease activity - broader than this review's existing off-flavor/chlorophyll focus, and directly relevant to why some leaves (cassava's cyanogenic glycosides, per cassava_foodchem2023) are harder matrices than others.
2. **Table 4 (commercial LPC producers)**: names specific companies already selling leaf protein concentrate commercially - Leaf Protein Co., Leaft Foods, RuBisCO Foods (claims 50-65% purity), Plantible Foods, and Proleri (claims 60-80% purity). This is the first evidence found in this entire review that commercial-scale leaf protein products already exist at purity levels squarely inside or above this project's 50-60% target band - a useful competitive-landscape data point not previously in gaps_and_opportunities.md.
3. **Direct carrot-leaf citation**: "heating carrot leaf juice might result in an LPC with a less pronounced green flavor but a slightly cooked taste (Nawaz et al. 2024)." This is one of only a small handful of carrot-specific findings in the entire literature surfaced across this whole review (alongside carrot_greenleaves_pmc's fractionation-failure data and carrot_composition_baseline's composition data) and should be surfaced prominently in synthesis_report.md - it directly speaks to the off-flavor axis of the target specs and suggests heat treatment on carrot leaf specifically trades "green" for "cooked" flavor rather than eliminating off-flavor outright, consistent with this review's broader finding (from Vatansever et al., now offflavor_mechanism_cluster) that heat/thermal treatment reduces but rarely eliminates off-flavor compounds.
4. **Full reference list** was reviewed for additional papers not yet in this review's citation set; no clearly new, directly-relevant primary papers beyond what's already covered were identified (the review's own primary sources substantially overlap with papers already obtained independently in this project, e.g. martin2014_spinach-type chromatography approaches, duckweed and alfalfa UF work).

**Overall framing statement (abstract-level, now confirmed in full text):** the review explicitly frames the state of the field as: enzymatic hydrolysis, ultrasound-assisted extraction, and membrane filtration are the three main technologies currently improving yield/purity/functionality, but low yields, high processing cost, and persistent undesirable sensory attributes (green color, earthy/off flavors) remain the primary barriers to commercial adoption - i.e., exactly the four-way trade-off (purity, yield, color, flavor) this project is trying to solve simultaneously, now confirmed as the field's own stated central challenge rather than just this project's framing.
